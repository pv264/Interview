
## 1. What are Logs, Metrics, and Traces in Observability?

**Answer:**
In **observability**, **logs**, **metrics**, and **traces** are the three main ways we monitor and troubleshoot applications, especially in **microservices environments**.

### 1. Metrics
* **Metrics** are numerical values collected over time, like CPU usage, memory usage, request count, error rate, or response time. 
* They help us understand the overall health and performance of the system. 
* Metrics are mainly used for **dashboards, alerting, and scaling decisions**. For example, in **AWS Auto Scaling**, we may scale out **EC2 instances** when CPU or request count increases.

### 2. Logs
* **Logs** are detailed event records generated by applications or infrastructure. 
* They tell us exactly what happened at a specific time. 
* Logs are mainly used for **debugging and root-cause analysis**. For example, if an API fails, logs may show database timeout errors or authentication failures.

### 3. Traces
* **Traces** are used mostly in distributed or microservice architectures. 
* A trace follows a single request as it travels across multiple services. It helps identify where latency or failures occur in the request flow. 
* For example, a request may go through **API Gateway → Haystack → vLLM → Milvus DB**, and tracing helps identify which service is slowing down.

---

### In simple terms:
* **Metrics** tell us **there is a problem**.
* **Traces** tell us **where the problem is**.
* **Logs** tell us **why the problem happened**.

### Real-World Application:
In my projects, I used **Prometheus** and **Grafana** for metrics monitoring, and I also worked with **Jaeger** for distributed tracing in microservices environments.

> **Senior Signal:** The modern industry standard is rapidly moving towards **OpenTelemetry** (OTel). Mentioning OTel in an interview shows forward-thinking, as it provides a single set of APIs and agents to capture all three pillars (Logs, Metrics, and Traces) in a unified way, rather than instrumenting applications with separate tools for each.
## 2. Explain about p50, p95, and p99 latency percentiles

**Answer:**
In performance monitoring and observability, **percentiles** are used to measure latency (response time) much more accurately than averages. They tell us how long a specific percentage of requests took to process.

### 1. p50 (Median Latency)
* **What it is:** **50%** of the requests are processed in this time or faster, and 50% take longer. 
* **Why it matters:** It represents the **typical user experience**. If your p50 is 100ms, half of your users are experiencing load times of 100ms or less.

### 2. p95 (Tail Latency)
* **What it is:** **95%** of the requests are processed in this time or faster. Only 5% of requests are slower.
* **Why it matters:** It highlights the experience of the **slowest 5% of your users**. It helps identify edge cases, such as slow database queries or minor network bottlenecks, that don't affect the majority of users but still degrade the experience for a noticeable chunk of traffic.

### 3. p99 (Extreme Tail Latency)
* **What it is:** **99%** of requests are processed in this time or faster. Only 1% of requests are slower.
* **Why it matters:** This tracks the **absolute worst user experiences**. Spikes in p99 latency often indicate underlying system stress, such as garbage collection (GC) pauses, CPU throttling, or heavy lock contention in a database like Milvus or RDS.

---

### Why not just use "Average" (Mean)?
Averages are dangerous in observability because they hide the outliers. If 99 requests take 10ms, but 1 request takes 5,000ms (5 seconds), the average will look perfectly fine, completely hiding the fact that a user had a terrible experience. Percentiles expose those hidden failures.

> **Senior Signal:** In enterprise SLA (Service Level Agreement) discussions, we rarely promise a p50 latency. We almost always guarantee a **p95 or p99** threshold (e.g., "p99 response time will be under 200ms"). Showing that you understand *why* averages lie and why p99 matters for capacity planning and auto-scaling decisions is a huge plus in SRE/DevOps interviews!
